Most of us live near people we barely know. We share a postcode, a fence, sometimes a polite nod, and we call that a neighbourhood. It is not the same thing as a community, and somewhere along the way most of us stopped expecting it to be.

Distributed Republic is our attempt to take that expectation seriously again. It is not a product, and it is not an invitation to join a commune. It is a values-led project, a journey we are on, and this site is the place where we share it with anyone on a similar one.

## What we mean by community

There is a spectrum that runs from "located near each other" all the way to a genuinely shared life. What moves you along it is not architecture. It is overlap: meals eaten together on purpose, holidays celebrated together, taking turns with the children, time given deliberately rather than incidentally. Community has to feel alive. It is practised, not declared.

That is the standard we are holding ourselves to. The village we are after is a support network, not a postcode.

## People first, then places

It is tempting to start with land. Find the site, draw the plans, and assume the rest will follow. We think that gets it backwards.

Communities that work are built on the right people, trust, and the right evolution over time. They often outgrow their first home and move on together, which tells you something important: the community was never the building. So our exploration starts with people and the agreements between them, and lets the places follow.

## Agreements, not rules

We describe the model as anarchic agreements, which sounds more dramatic than it is. It means the way a community governs itself is written together, by the people living it, as a collective agreement rather than a rulebook handed down. We are deliberately not pinning those agreements down here, because writing them is the community's first shared act, not ours to pre-empt.

The same goes for daily life. Shared practices can be part of a community's culture, but they are not conditions of belonging. What matters is openness, not obligation.

## One village, then a network

A single community is a handful of households living closely, sharing meals, work, and ideally a cooperative venture that helps hold it together. The longer ambition is a network: communities linked to one another, sharing knowledge, support, and opportunity while each keeps its own character. The cooperative tradition gives us plenty to learn from here, including Mondragon, where a federation of member-owned businesses grew from one community.

## Where you come in

People join a journey like this at different distances. Some want to be in the car, some travel in the convoy, and plenty are happy watching from home to see how it goes. All of those are welcome.

This blog is where we will write down what we learn: the ideas we are testing, the places and communities that inspire us, and the honest parts where things prove harder than expected. If any of it helps you picture your own version of community living, it is doing its job.
